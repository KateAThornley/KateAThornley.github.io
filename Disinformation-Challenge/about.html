THE PROBLEM

In consuming news and content online, users tend to prefer content that aligns with and reaffirms their existing beliefs, this is known as the echo chamber effect (Jamieson & Cappella, 2008; Sunstein, 2002). The echo chamber effect pushes people into silos and exacerbates polarization, particularly with the rise of online news consumption and social media. The tendency to fall into an echo chamber can be fed by personal preference and habits (Dubois & Blank, 2018), or by filter bubbles- which is when news aggregators’ and social media platforms’ algorithms only serve you content which affirms your existing belief (Pariser, 2011). When users are stuck in echo chambers online, they are more susceptible to disinformation because citizens lack a common understanding of shared facts (citation needed). By having their own beliefs reconfirmed and echoed back to them, users also don’t develop and practice the critical thinking skills necessary for identifying and avoiding misinformation (citation needed).

THE APPROACH

At KickBait, our approach to countering disinformation online is to challenge the echo chambers that users find themselves in. We have done this by creating an educational tool which allows users to situate their own news consumption habits within the Canadian media ecosystem, based on the level of political polarization of the content they consume and its credibility. This tool encourages critical thinking and creates an opportunity for users to critically reflect on their online news consumption habits. We believe that if users can recognize that they are in an echo chamber, they will be better equipped to take actions to get out the echo chamber. This tool encourages users to develop positive habits to get out of echo chambers including: Reading content they disagree with; Checking a news source that’s different from what they normally read; and Trying to confirm political information by searching online for another source (Dubois, 2017).

THE METHODOLOGY

We believe transparency in how this tool has been created is essential. It is necessary to share the methodology for ranking within each category, so reasonable people of differing political beliefs can rank the same sources and come up with similar results.

METHODOLOGY FOR THE DEMO

For the map we have presented here, we selected 25 online news media sources based on their overall popularity in the Canadian news ecosystem as well as their political distribution. This selection is based on a 2019 study on political polarization in Canada led by Dr. Taylor Owens.
We then used an existing media ranking resource called Media Bias/ Fact Check to provide a ranking for each source, based on its level of political partisanship and credibility.

Media Bias/ Fact Check provides a ranking of political partisanship based on the following four criteria:

  • Biased words/ headline: Does the source use loaded words to convey emotion to sway the reader. Do headlines match the story.
  • Factual/Sourcing: Does the source report factually and back up claims with well sourced evidence.
  • Story Choices: Does the source report news from both sides or do they only publish one side.
  • Political Affiliation: How strongly does the source endorse a particular political ideology. How extreme are their views?

Political Partisanship rankings were distributed according to left and right political ideology. This approach has various limitations and challenges: positions that are considered on the left or right of the political spectrum change over time, there isn’t always a “centre” on each topic, definitions or liberal and conservative are contested between individuals, and its contested whether there are more dimensions to consider than just liberal/ conservative (Otero, 2018).

Our aim with this tool is not to engage with political ideological debate, but to encourage positive media consumption habits. To address this limitation, we adjusted the political partisanship ranking to be from neutral to extremely partisan. In this way, both extreme left and extreme right content will fall on the same side of the map. We believe the issue isn’t in what side of the political spectrum you fall on, but how neutral your news sources are.

Media Bias/ Fact Check provides a ranking of credibility based on the factual reporting score above. Based on the rankings provided by Media Bias/ Fact Check, we assigned each source a score out of ten for political partisanship and credibility to place it on our map.

We recognize several possible issues in methodology followed by Media Bias/ Fact Check. For example, with a single organization conducting the ranking, its own internal biases can inform its interpretation of the content. Further, many of the measures outlined above, particularly political affiliation can be highly subjective.

A few of the Canadian sites included in our list were not available on Media Bias/ Fact Check, so we ranked them ourselves, following the criteria outlined by Media Bias/ Fact Check.

However, this resource was valuable to provide a baseline rating system for us to build a demo of how our tool will work. More work will need to be done on mapping news sources in the Canadian context, which we outline below.

FURTHER CONSIDERATIONS

The rankings we have provided here are preliminary, as a means to demonstrate how the tool works. Moving forward, we plan to learn from the methodology of similar organizations to provide nuanced and credible rankings for news media in Canada.

Extensive work is being done on this in the American context, by organizations such as AllSides’ Media Bias Chart and Ad Fonte Media’s Media Bias Chart. Both of these organizations have extensively researched and ranked hundreds of news outlets and mapped them according to political partisanship. However, in Canada we are just starting to catch up.

AllSides ranks only media bias, not the accuracy or credibility of facts. They use multiple methods to determine the bias of a source including by asking the following questions

  • What’s your bias?
  • What’s the bias of this article?
  • How does every “bias group” rate this article?
  • What’s the average bias rating of several articles from this source?

They also use blind bias surveys, editorial review, third party analysis, and independent reviews to review the bias of a source. By combining multiple methods, they can provide a more credible and objective rating.

Ad Fonte conducts complex analysis of the content of stories from each source using individual sentences as the unit of analysis. They present the ranking of each news source as a weighted average of this analysis. As news articles continue to be published and analysed, the position of a source on the chart will move over time. To measure the quality of news, they use three metrics:

  • Veracity: based on fact checkers
  • Expression: based on syntax and intent of sentence
  • Fairness: based on Federal Rules of Evidence, which a judge uses to rule on objections in court

To measure the political partisanship of news, they consider both the political position bias (how extreme the idea or policy is), and linguistic expression bias (the degree to which an article promotes a political position through linguistic rhetoric).
